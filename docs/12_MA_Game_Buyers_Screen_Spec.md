\# The M\&A Rainmaker

\## Buyers Screen Specification

\### Version 1.0



---



\# 1. Purpose



This document defines the detailed screen specification for the \*\*Buyers\*\* screen in \*\*The M\&A Rainmaker\*\*.



The Buyers screen is one of the central strategic screens of the game.



It should function as:



\- the main control surface for the buyer universe,

\- the primary screen for tracking bidder behaviour,

\- the place where competitive tension becomes visible,

\- the comparison engine for interest, seriousness and execution credibility,

\- and a live strategic map of who matters, who is bluffing, and who may become dangerous.



This is not a CRM list with better lighting.

This is the theatre of competitive process management.



The screen must help the player understand:



\- who the key buyers are,

\- how they are evolving,

\- where leverage is building,

\- where illusion is masquerading as interest,

\- and how shortlist and process strategy should respond.



---



\# 2. Screen Design Intent



The Buyers screen should feel like:



\- a strategic intelligence console,

\- a competitive process map,

\- a premium bidder-management terminal,

\- and a disciplined comparison surface for ambiguity under pressure.



Visually, it should sit primarily between:



\- \*\*Terminal Prestige\*\*, for analytical density, matrix logic and signal reading,

\- and \*\*Outlook Noir\*\*, for clarity, usability and professional workflow structure.



A lighter \*\*Cinematic Dealroom\*\* influence may appear in:

\- selected buyer summary blocks,

\- major shortlist moments,

\- and high-consequence comparison states.



The mood should be:



\- analytical,

\- tense,

\- alive,

\- controlled,

\- and slightly predatory in the civilised M\&A sense.



The player should feel they are reading people through process behaviour, not just staring at static profiles.



---



\# 3. Gameplay Role



\# 3.1 Core Role

The Buyers screen is one of the main engines of strategic gameplay.



It performs six major functions:



1\. tracks the buyer universe,

2\. compares buyer attractiveness and risk,

3\. supports shortlist decisions,

4\. surfaces behavioural signals,

5\. shows momentum and deterioration over time,

6\. connects buyer actions to broader process management.



\# 3.2 Why It Matters

A sell-side process only becomes truly alive when buyers stop being names on a list and start behaving like competing actors with different motives, strengths and pathologies.



This screen is where that becomes legible.



Without a strong Buyers screen, the process risks feeling like:

\- documents moving around,

\- tasks being completed,

\- and nobody actually wanting to buy anything.



That would be a rather expensive spreadsheet in costume.



\# 3.3 What the Player Should Feel

The player should feel:



\- they are managing a competitive field,

\- they are reading intent from partial evidence,

\- they can create or lose leverage,

\- and every buyer is a bundle of opportunity, ego, friction and execution risk.



---



\# 4. Primary Player Questions



The Buyers screen must answer these questions quickly:



\- Which buyers matter most right now?

\- Who is serious versus merely curious?

\- Who is likely to pay well?

\- Who is likely to execute reliably?

\- Which buyers are improving or deteriorating?

\- Who belongs in the shortlist?

\- Where is competitive tension strongest?

\- Which buyer signals should be trusted, and which should be treated with suspicion?



If the player cannot distinguish signal from posture on this screen, the screen has failed.



---



\# 5. Functional Objectives



The Buyers screen must support all of the following:



\- viewing the full buyer universe,

\- filtering and sorting buyers,

\- comparing buyers across multiple dimensions,

\- visualising momentum and process behaviour,

\- tracking shortlist logic,

\- surfacing red flags,

\- linking buyers to messages, tasks, risks and timeline state,

\- helping the player choose where to invest time and political capital,

\- and making buyer ambiguity legible without eliminating uncertainty.



---



\# 6. Screen Architecture



\# 6.1 Canonical Layout



The Buyers screen should sit inside the standard application shell:



\- Topbar

\- Sidebar

\- Buyers Workspace



Inside the Buyers Workspace:



\- Workspace Header

\- Buyers Control Bar

\- View Mode Bar

\- Main Buyers Area

\- Optional Comparison Strip

\- Optional Right Detail Panel or integrated detail column



\# 6.2 Main Structural Logic



The screen should support a \*\*2-zone primary layout\*\*:



\- Main Buyer Universe Zone

\- Selected Buyer Detail Zone



This is the correct structural model because the player needs both:

\- broad comparative visibility,

\- and focused detail on a chosen bidder.



A third supporting layer, optional but useful:

\- Multi-buyer comparison strip or modal.



\# 6.3 Default Desktop Layout



Recommended distribution:



\- Buyer Universe Zone: 62%

\- Buyer Detail Zone: 38%



This balance gives enough space for list/matrix views while preserving proper detail.



If Heatmap mode is selected, the detail panel may collapse or move to drawer behaviour.

The Buyers screen should support four core view modes:



1\. List View

2\. Card View

3\. Comparison Matrix View

4\. Heatmap View



Each has a different job.

That is good system design, not indecision.

\# 10. List View



\# 10.1 Purpose

Primary operational mode for scanning and comparing many buyers efficiently.



\# 10.2 Layout

\# 10.3 Recommended Columns

Core columns:

\- Buyer Name

\- Type

\- Geography

\- Interest Level

\- Valuation Posture

\- Chemistry

\- Execution Credibility

\- Process Status

\- Last Activity

\- Red Flags

\- Shortlist Marker



Optional later:

\- Financing Confidence

\- DD Complexity

\- Strategic Fit

\- Political Sensitivity



\# 10.4 Column Behaviour

\- sortable

\- hover states

\- row selectable

\# 10.5 Strengths

Best for:

\- large universes,

\- efficient scanning,

\- process administration,

\- shortlist refinement.

This table must be crisp, readable and analytically elegant.

Dark UI tables become sludge very easily. Resist the swamp.

\- DD Risk

\# 14. Buyer Detail Zone



\# 14.1 Purpose

Provide focused analysis of a selected buyer.



\# 14.2 Structure

The selected buyer detail panel should contain:



1\. Buyer Header

2\. Strategic Summary

3\. Core Scoring Block

4\. Process Status

5\. Behavioural Signals

6\. Risks \& Frictions

7\. Linked Activity

8\. Action Area



\# 14.3 Buyer Header

Should include:

\- buyer name

\- type

\- geography

\- current process phase

\- shortlist state

\- key status chips



Optional:

\- sector fit tag

\- strategic rationale hint



\# 14.4 Strategic Summary

A short interpretation block explaining the buyer’s current profile.



Example:

\- `Strong strategic logic and credible execution capability, but increasing signs of process-control behaviour and likely DD intensity.`



This summary is crucial.

Raw metrics alone are not enough.



\# 14.5 Core Scoring Block

Display core dimensions clearly.



Recommended dimensions:

\- Interest Level

\- Valuation Potential

\- Seriousness

\- Execution Credibility

\- Chemistry with Seller

\- Financing Confidence

\- DD Complexity

\- Re-trade Risk



These can be shown as:

\- bars,

\- scored labels,

\- chips,

\- or compact indicator rows.



\# 14.6 Process Status

Show:

\- current stage

\- last interaction

\- next expected action

\- momentum trend

\- timeline sensitivity



\# 14.7 Behavioural Signals

This is one of the most important areas of the screen.



Show evidence of behaviour such as:

\- fast responsiveness

\- evasive requests

\- insistence on exclusivity

\- increasing information appetite

\- inconsistent tone

\- strong enthusiasm

\- superficial curiosity

\- low internal alignment

\- corporate bureaucracy drag

\- advisor-led process sophistication



This area helps the player read intent through behaviour.



\# 14.8 Risks \& Frictions

Surface key concerns such as:

\- financing uncertainty

\- likely price pressure later

\- difficult integration politics

\- founder chemistry weakness

\- legal complexity

\- overbearing DD style

\- timing unreliability

\- internal buyer politics



\# 14.9 Linked Activity

Show relevant linked items:

\- recent inbox messages

\- open tasks

\- active risks

\- pending deliverables

\- recent milestone interactions



\# 14.10 Action Area

Core actions may include:

\- Prioritise

\- Deprioritise

\- Add to Shortlist

\- Remove from Shortlist

\- Compare

\- Open Inbox Messages

\- Open Buyer Risks

\- Add Internal Note

\- Schedule Follow-up, if system supports

\# 16. Buyer Scoring Dimensions



\# 16.1 Design Principle

The game should not collapse buyer evaluation into a single master score.



That would be neat, and wrong.



Buyers in M\&A are multidimensional.

A buyer can be:

\- highly interested,

\- very credible,

\- poor on chemistry,

\- excellent on speed,

\- dangerous on re-trade.



That complexity is the point.



\# 16.2 Recommended Dimensions

Use at least the following dimensions:



\- Interest

\- Valuation Potential

\- Seriousness

\- Execution Credibility

\- Chemistry

\- Financing Confidence

\- DD Complexity

\- Re-trade Risk

\- Process Control Behaviour

\- Strategic Fit



\# 16.3 Dimension Presentation

Each dimension should use clear visual language:

\- Low / Medium / High

\- numeric band

\- bars

\- chips

\- or compact scale labels



Consistency matters more than cleverness.



\# 16.4 Hidden vs Visible Logic

Some dimension values may be:

\- fully visible,

\- partially visible,

\- or inferred through behaviour.



This is desirable.

The player should sometimes work with imperfect knowledge.

\# 17. Shortlist Mechanics



\# 17.1 Purpose

The shortlist is one of the major strategic filters in the process.



\# 17.2 Shortlist States

Recommended states:

\- Not Considered

\- Active

\- Watchlist

\- Shortlisted

\- Advanced

\- Dropped



\# 17.3 Shortlist Actions

The player should be able to:

\- add buyer to shortlist,

\- remove buyer from shortlist,

\- compare shortlisted buyers,

\- view shortlist-only mode,

\- see shortlist capacity pressure.



\# 17.4 Shortlist Constraints

The game may impose practical constraints:

\- shortlist limited to a target number,

\- shortlist quality affects process intensity,

\- weak shortlisting reduces competitive tension,

\- over-expanded shortlisting strains execution and seller energy.



\# 17.5 Shortlist Visibility

The screen should clearly show:

\- which buyers are shortlisted,

\- why they matter,

\- and what trade-offs shortlist composition creates.



This is where process design becomes gameplay.

\# 18. Buyer Behavioural Signals



\# 18.1 Purpose

Signals should make buyers feel alive and strategically interpretable.



\# 18.2 Signal Types

\- fast turnaround

\- warm language

\- cold formalism

\- high question density

\- selective engagement

\- aggressive timetable pressure

\- repeated requests for exclusivity

\- inconsistent messaging

\- management enthusiasm

\- advisor-driven professionalism

\- opportunistic behaviour

\- defensive legal posture



\# 18.3 Signal Use

\- player interpretation,

\- risk assessment,

\# 18.4 Ambiguity Rule

Signals should not always map cleanly to truth.



Example:

\- fast responses may indicate strong interest,

\- or simply a very efficient associate with no real conviction behind them.



Excellent. That is where the fun lives.

\# 19. Buyer Risks and Red Flags



\# 19.1 Purpose

The screen must help the player identify danger early.



\# 19.2 Typical Buyer Red Flags

Examples:

\- superficial enthusiasm

\- weak financing confidence

\- likely price retrade

\- DD overreach

\- slow internal approvals

\- weak chemistry with founder

\- unclear strategic rationale

\- political sensitivities

\- excessive process control attempts

\- low probability of board approval

\- integration credibility gap



\# 19.3 Red Flag Presentation

Red flags should appear:

\- in the buyer row/card,

\- in buyer detail,

\- in shortlist comparison,

\- and in risk-linked flows.



\# 19.4 Red Flag Rule

A red flag should not merely exist as metadata.

It should influence gameplay.

\# 21. Dynamic Behaviour



\# 21.1 Buyer Evolution Over Time

Buyers must visibly evolve.



Possible changes:

\- rising interest

\- fading seriousness

\- stronger valuation ambition

\- worsening process behaviour

\- improved seller chemistry

\- deteriorating credibility

\- movement into shortlist

\- withdrawal or drop-off



\# 21.2 Phase Sensitivity

Different phases should emphasise different dimensions.



\## Early Phase

More emphasis on:

\- outreach response

\- initial interest

\- strategic fit

\- curiosity vs seriousness



\## Mid Phase

More emphasis on:

\- valuation posture

\- credibility

\- chemistry

\- DD appetite

\- shortlist readiness



\## Late Phase

More emphasis on:

\- execution credibility

\- financing confidence

\- re-trade risk

\- legal posture

\- timetable discipline



\# 21.3 Event Sensitivity

Major events should change buyer state:

\- bad market news

\- management presentation

\- DD issues

\- client pressure

\- competitor moves

\- leaked process tension

\- exclusivity requests



The screen must visibly reflect these shifts.

\- muted comparison placeholders

\- restrained shimmer if needed



\# 22.5 High-Pressure State

\- more signals visible

\- shortlist markers prominent

\- risk flags sharper

---



\# 23. Accessibility and Readability



\# 23.1 Contrast

\- scores and statuses must remain readable

\- shortlisted vs non-shortlisted must be obvious

\- risk markers must not rely only on colour

\- table text must remain crisp in dark UI



\# 23.2 Comparison Clarity

Matrix and heatmap views must support interpretation without perfect colour perception.



\# 23.3 Click Targets

\- rows

\- cards

\- shortlist toggles

\- compare selectors

must all be comfortably usable.



\# 23.4 Information Overload Rule

The screen should help the player judge buyers, not drown them in ornamental metadata.



---



\# 24. Visual Style Guidance for This Screen



\# 24.1 Dominant Variant

The Buyers screen should primarily use \*\*Terminal Prestige\*\*.



\# 24.2 Supporting Variant

Use \*\*Outlook Noir\*\* for:

\- structure,

\- filter usability,

\- detail readability,

\- action clarity.



\# 24.3 Limited Cinematic Accent

A small amount of \*\*Cinematic Dealroom\*\* may be used in:

\- shortlisted buyer highlight states,

\- milestone-sensitive buyer summaries,

\- and major comparison moments.



\# 24.4 Visual Rules

\- analytical density

\- disciplined paneling

\- clean row/card rhythm

\- strong contrast

\- restrained accent usage

\- shortlist state clearly visible

\- signal colour used for meaning, not decoration



This screen should feel like a serious advisory instrument with a hidden appetite for drama.
