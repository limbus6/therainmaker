\# The M\&A Rainmaker

\## Risks \& Issues Screen Specification

\### Version 1.0



---



\# 1. Purpose



This document defines the detailed screen specification for the \*\*Risks \& Issues\*\* screen in \*\*The M\&A Rainmaker\*\*.



The Risks \& Issues screen is the main control surface for transaction fragility, uncertainty, escalation and mitigation.



It should function as:



\- the structured risk register of the process,

\- the primary screen for identifying and monitoring deal threats,

\- the place where visible and emerging issues are tracked,

\- the system through which mitigation decisions become meaningful,

\- and the layer that makes uncertainty operational rather than decorative.



This is not a panic board.

This is a professional transaction risk management interface.



The screen must help the player understand:



\- what can go wrong,

\- what is already going wrong,

\- how severe each issue is,

\- how likely escalation is,

\- what is hidden versus known,

\- what mitigation exists,

\- and where unresolved fragility threatens the deal.



---



\# 2. Screen Design Intent



The Risks \& Issues screen should feel like:



\- a disciplined transaction risk console,

\- a clean escalation management surface,

\- a sober register of deal fragility,

\- and a place where danger is made legible without melodrama.



Visually, it should sit primarily between:



\- \*\*Terminal Prestige\*\*, for analytical density, severity logic and structured register design,

\- and \*\*Outlook Noir\*\*, for legibility, navigation and decision clarity.



A limited \*\*Cinematic Dealroom\*\* influence may appear only in:

\- major escalation states,

\- deal-threatening issue summaries,

\- and milestone-critical risk moments.



The mood should be:



\- controlled,

\- unsentimental,

\- tense,

\- clear,

\- and quietly alarming in the proper professional way.



This screen should feel like the room where optimism is audited.



---



\# 3. Gameplay Role



\# 3.1 Core Role

The Risks \& Issues screen is one of the main systems that gives the game teeth.



It performs seven major functions:



1\. tracks active risks,

2\. distinguishes risk from realised issue,

3\. surfaces severity and probability,

4\. supports mitigation planning,

5\. links risk to buyers, client, team, deliverables and timing,

6\. makes hidden fragility progressively visible,

7\. and turns uncertainty into strategic gameplay.



\# 3.2 Why It Matters

Without a proper risk system, the game becomes too orderly.



Real processes are shaped not only by:

\- timing,

\- buyer management,

\- and materials,



but by:

\- hidden tensions,

\- diligence surprises,

\- legal friction,

\- client mood shifts,

\- buyer behaviour changes,

\- financing uncertainty,

\- and operational slippage.



This screen is where that uncertainty becomes governable, though never fully tame.



\# 3.3 What the Player Should Feel

The player should feel:



\- that risk is always present,

\- that not all risks deserve equal attention,

\- that mitigation matters,

\- that some risks remain partially hidden until triggered,

\- and that ignoring small exposures can produce very expensive future theatre.



---



\# 4. Primary Player Questions



The Risks \& Issues screen must answer these questions quickly:



\- What are the main threats to the deal right now?

\- Which risks are rising?

\- Which risks have already become issues?

\- What is severe, and what is merely noisy?

\- What can be mitigated?

\- What is linked to a buyer, client, workstream or milestone?

\- Which unresolved issue could derail the process next?



If the player cannot distinguish existential threat from background friction, the screen has failed.



---



\# 5. Functional Objectives



The Risks \& Issues screen must support all of the following:



\- full risk register visibility,

\- issue register visibility,

\- severity and probability tracking,

\- trend tracking,

\- mitigation action tracking,

\- ownership and escalation tracking,

\- hidden-versus-visible logic,

\- links to all core systems,

\- and clear prioritisation of what deserves intervention.

\# 6. Screen Architecture



\# 6.1 Canonical Layout



The Risks \& Issues screen should sit inside the standard application shell:



\- Topbar

\- Sidebar

\- Risks Workspace



Inside the Risks Workspace:



\- Workspace Header

\- Risks Control Bar

\- View Mode Bar

\- Main Risk Universe Area

\- Selected Risk / Issue Detail Zone

\- Optional Bottom Escalation Strip



\# 6.2 Main Structural Logic



The screen should support a \*\*2-zone primary layout\*\*:



\- Main Risk Universe Zone

\- Selected Risk / Issue Detail Zone



Supporting layer:

\- optional bottom strip for escalating, unresolved or deal-critical items



\# 6.3 Default Desktop Layout



Recommended width distribution:



\- Risk Universe Zone: 60%

\- Risk / Issue Detail Zone: 40%



This gives enough space for the register while preserving room for real diagnostic detail.

\# 16. Risk vs Issue Logic



\# 16.1 Purpose

The game should distinguish between:

\- a potential threat,

\- and a realised problem.



\# 16.2 Definitions

\## Risk

A potential future negative event or exposure.



\## Issue

A risk that has materialised or is actively disrupting the process.



\# 16.3 Example

\- `Buyer financing uncertainty` is a risk.

\- `Buyer informs advisor financing approval will be delayed` is an issue.



\# 16.4 Gameplay Relevance

This distinction matters because:

\- risks invite anticipation and mitigation,

\- issues demand response and containment.



\# 16.5 Design Rule

The transition from risk to issue should be visible and meaningful.

This is one of the game’s most important dramatic mechanisms.



---



\# 17. Severity, Probability and Trend



\# 17.1 Purpose

These dimensions allow structured judgement.



\# 17.2 Recommended States

\- Low

\- High

\- Critical



Probability:

\- Unlikely

\- Possible

\- Likely

\- Imminent



Trend:

\- Falling

\- Stable

\- Rising

\- Accelerating



\# 17.3 Design Rule

Do not collapse everything into a single score.

A likely nuisance and a rare catastrophe are different species of problem.



\# 17.4 Presentation

Use:

\- chips

\- labelled scales

\- icons

\- small trend arrows



All must remain readable in dark UI and must not rely only on colour.

\# 18. Hidden vs Visible Risk Logic



\# 18.1 Purpose

The game should preserve uncertainty and discovery.



\# 18.2 Core Principle

Not all risks should be visible from the start.



Some risks may be:

\- fully visible,

\- partially inferred,

\- or hidden until triggered by:

&nbsp; - events

&nbsp; - messages

&nbsp; - DD findings

&nbsp; - buyer behaviour

&nbsp; - missed tasks

&nbsp; - market developments



\# 18.3 Visible Forms

A partially visible risk may appear as:

\- weak signal

\- rumour

\- concern marker

\- ambiguous buyer behaviour

\- unexplained delay

\- low-confidence note



\# 18.4 Design Rule

The player should sometimes sense exposure before they can fully name it.

That uncertainty is useful and realistic.

\# 19. Mitigation System



\# 19.1 Purpose

Risk management must be playable.



\# 19.2 Mitigation Actions

Mitigation may include:

\- creating task chains

\- accelerating review

\- informing client

\- replacing buyer emphasis

\- improving materials

\- reallocating team capacity

\- seeking clarification

\- delaying milestone

\- tightening shortlist

\- escalating legal support



\# 19.3 Mitigation Attributes

Each mitigation should have:

\- owner

\- cost or trade-off

\- progress

\- likely effectiveness

\- deadline or review point



\# 19.4 Trade-off Logic

Mitigation should sometimes reduce one exposure while worsening another.



Example:

\- accelerate DD response to preserve buyer confidence,

\- but overload team and create quality risk elsewhere.



That is the good machinery.

\# 22. Dynamic Behaviour



\# 22.1 Risk Evolution Over Time

Risks must visibly evolve.



Possible changes:

\- emerging

\- rising

\- stabilising

\- escalating

\- becoming issues

\- being contained

\- being resolved

\- being replaced by new exposures



\# 22.2 Phase Sensitivity

Different phases should emphasise different risk families.



\## Early Phase

More emphasis on:

\- client alignment

\- material readiness

\- outreach timing

\- buyer coverage quality



\## Mid Phase

More emphasis on:

\- buyer seriousness

\- shortlist quality

\- DD preparation

\- process control risk



\## Late Phase

More emphasis on:

\- legal

\- financing

\- exclusivity pressure

\- timetable compression

\- re-trade risk

\- closing fragility



\# 22.3 Event Sensitivity

Major events should create or intensify risk:

\- weak bid reception

\- buyer drop-out

\- DD issue

\- legal comment escalation

\- market volatility

\- founder reaction

\- internal overload



The screen should reflect this with visible consequences.

\# 24.1 Standard States

The Risks \& Issues screen must support:

\- full loaded state

\- no risk selected

\- no filter results

\- calm state with low active exposure

\- escalation-heavy state

\- loading state



\# 24.2 No Risk Selected State

Example:

\- `Select a risk or issue to review exposure, linked entities and mitigation.`



\# 24.3 No Filter Results State

Example:

\- `No risks match current filters.`



\# 24.4 Calm State

If exposures are relatively limited:

\- present fewer alerts

\- show stable summary text

\- preserve sense of vigilance



\# 24.5 Escalation-Heavy State

Later or crisis phases may show:

\- denser issue register

\- sharper trend markers

\- more active mitigations

\- more prominent critical items



Clarity must survive the storm.

\# 25.1 Contrast

\- severity, probability and trend must remain readable

\- issue state must not rely only on colour

\- heatmap interpretation must remain accessible



\# 25.2 Click Targets

Rows, heatmap points, mitigation links and actions must all be comfortably usable.



\# 25.3 Hierarchy

The player should rapidly find:

\- critical items,

\- rising risks,

\- unresolved issues,

\- and mitigation gaps.



\# 25.4 Information Overload Rule

The screen should support judgement, not perform bureaucratic dread for its own sake.

\# 26. Visual Style Guidance for This Screen



\# 26.1 Dominant Variant

The Risks \& Issues screen should primarily use \*\*Terminal Prestige\*\*.



\# 26.2 Supporting Variant

Use \*\*Outlook Noir\*\* for:

\- table structure,

\- filter usability,

\- detail panel clarity,

\- and action controls.



\# 26.3 Limited Cinematic Accent

A small amount of \*\*Cinematic Dealroom\*\* may be used in:

\- deal-critical issue states,

\- phase-threatening escalation modules,

\- and major milestone risk summaries.



\# 26.4 Visual Rules

\- crisp structured register

\- strong severity logic

\- restrained accent usage

\- trend indicators clearly readable

\- issue states more prominent than latent risks

\- calm detail panel with high signal density



This screen should look like the place where the process tells the truth about itself.

\# 30. Final Screen Statement



The Risks \& Issues screen of \*\*The M\&A Rainmaker\*\* must function as a premium deal-fragility management system, making uncertainty, escalation and mitigation legible and actionable.
