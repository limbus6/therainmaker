\# The M\&A Rainmaker

\## Progression Events Screen Specification

\### Phase Transition Screen + Milestone Outcome Screen

\### Version 1.0



---



\# 1. Purpose



This document defines the detailed screen specification for the \*\*Progression Events Screens\*\* in \*\*The M\&A Rainmaker\*\*.



This combined specification covers two closely related screen types:



1\. \*\*Phase Transition Screen\*\*

2\. \*\*Milestone Outcome Screen\*\*



Together, these screens form the game’s main progression-feedback layer.



They should function as:



\- the primary screens for marking major progression beats,

\- the interface through which the player feels transition, consequence and escalation,

\- the layer that turns process movement into emotionally legible game feedback,

\- and the system for framing what just changed, why it matters and what comes next.



These are not decorative interstitials.

These are the game’s progression punctuation marks.



They must help the player understand:



\- what phase or milestone has just been reached,

\- what changed in practical terms,

\- what consequences now exist,

\- what new priorities have emerged,

\- and how the process has been altered by events.



---



\# 2. Screen Design Intent



These progression screens should feel like:



\- elegant, high-signal transition moments,

\- premium transaction-event overlays,

\- composed but consequential visual pauses,

\- and rare moments where the game steps back from workflow density and says: this mattered.



Visually, these screens should sit primarily between:



\- \*\*Cinematic Dealroom\*\*, for emotional framing, dramatic restraint and progression atmosphere,

\- \*\*Outlook Noir\*\*, for clarity and structure,

\- with selective \*\*Terminal Prestige\*\* support for data deltas, implications and state changes.



The mood should be:



\- elevated,

\- clear,

\- consequential,

\- restrained,

\- and memorable.



These screens should not feel noisy, overly gamey or melodramatic.

They should feel like the deal itself has changed state.



---



\# 3. Gameplay Role



\# 3.1 Core Role

The Progression Events screens perform six major functions:



1\. mark major process transitions,

2\. communicate milestone outcomes,

3\. summarise consequences,

4\. surface what is now different,

5\. reorient the player toward the next priorities,

6\. and create rhythm and emotional pacing inside the simulation.



\# 3.2 Why They Matter

Without these screens, the process risks feeling too continuous and operational.



A player may:

\- complete tasks,

\- handle buyers,

\- manage risks,

\- advance weeks,



but still not feel the structural meaning of progress.



These screens solve that problem.



They tell the player:

\- a chapter changed,

\- a gate was crossed,

\- a result landed,

\- something now matters more than before.



\# 3.3 What the Player Should Feel

The player should feel:



\- that the process is moving through distinct states,

\- that milestones have consequences,

\- that each phase alters the shape of the game,

\- and that outcomes are being absorbed into the system, not merely recorded.



---



\# 4. Combined Design Principle



The \*\*Phase Transition Screen\*\* and \*\*Milestone Outcome Screen\*\* should share the same design family, but serve different narrative functions.



\## Phase Transition Screen

Focus:

\- chapter change,

\- process reframing,

\- mechanic emphasis shift,

\- new risks and goals.



\## Milestone Outcome Screen

Focus:

\- event result,

\- consequence summary,

\- metric deltas,

\- immediate next implications.



Together, they should feel like:

\- strategic progression feedback,

\- not cutscenes,

\- not popups,

\- not bureaucratic notifications.



---



\# 5. Screen Type A. Phase Transition Screen



---



\# 5A.1 Purpose



The Phase Transition Screen marks entry into a new phase of the process.



It should communicate:



\- that the deal has entered a new chapter,

\- what this phase means,

\- what priorities now dominate,

\- what new mechanics or pressures are active,

\- and how the player should mentally reframe the process.



This is a controlled breath before the next compression begins.



---



\# 5A.2 Primary Player Questions



The Phase Transition Screen must answer these questions immediately:



\- What phase am I entering?

\- What changes now?

\- What are the main objectives of this phase?

\- What risks become more important?

\- What new mechanics, systems or tensions are now active?

\- How should I think differently going forward?



---



\# 5A.3 Functional Objectives



The Phase Transition Screen must support all of the following:



\- announce the new phase clearly,

\- summarise the logic of the phase,

\- communicate primary objectives,

\- communicate new pressures or constraints,

\- signal any unlocked systems or changed emphasis,

\- and orient the player toward immediate next action.



---



\# 5A.4 Screen Architecture



The Phase Transition Screen should behave as a \*\*full-screen overlay\*\* or \*\*full-workspace event panel\*\*.



Recommended structure:



1\. Phase Title Area

2\. Narrative Framing Block

3\. Objectives Block

4\. New Mechanics / Emphasis Block

5\. Risk / Caution Block

6\. Continue Action Area



Optional:

7\. Background process visual or animated atmosphere layer



This screen should interrupt normal navigation briefly and deliberately.



---



\# 5A.5 Phase Title Area



\## Content

\- Phase Number

\- Phase Name

\- optional short subtitle or tagline



Example:

\- `Phase 4`

\- `Management Interaction`

\- `The process now becomes personal.`



\## Design Rule

This title must carry authority and progression weight.



---



\# 5A.6 Narrative Framing Block



\## Purpose

Provide a concise explanation of what this phase represents in transaction terms.



\## Content

A short paragraph or two describing:

\- what is now happening,

\- how the process is changing,

\- and what kind of judgement the player now needs.



Example:

\- `The process now moves from broad market engagement into deeper buyer interaction. Management credibility, process discipline and seller confidence become more exposed. Momentum can build quickly, but so can buyer selectivity and internal tension.`



\## Design Rule

This should feel like elegant framing, not florid prose.

No purple smoke. No MBA haiku.



---



\# 5A.7 Objectives Block



\## Purpose

Tell the player what success looks like in the new phase.



\## Content

Show 3 to 5 core objectives.



Examples:

\- maintain buyer momentum

\- prepare management interaction carefully

\- protect seller confidence

\- avoid timetable drift

\- surface weak buyers early



\## Behaviour

Objectives should be:

\- clear

\- phase-specific

\- strategically useful

\- optionally linked to screens or systems



---



\# 5A.8 New Mechanics / Emphasis Block



\## Purpose

Show what the player should pay more attention to now.



\## Content

Examples:

\- management presentation readiness now matters

\- buyer chemistry becomes more visible

\- DD readiness risk begins to build

\- shortlist logic starts to take shape

\- client expectation sensitivity increases



If new systems are actually unlocked in gameplay, show them here.



\## Design Rule

Do not overload this block.

The point is to sharpen focus, not to deliver a software release note funeral.



---



\# 5A.9 Risk / Caution Block



\## Purpose

Surface the main dangers of the phase.



\## Content

Examples:

\- weak buyer engagement may now become visible

\- management fatigue can damage momentum

\- internal alignment gaps may widen under ambiguity

\- review delays may compress the next phase



This block should help the player enter the phase with eyes open.



---



\# 5A.10 Continue Action Area



\## Purpose

Allow the player to proceed back into the game.



\## Content

Primary action:

\- `Enter Phase`

or

\- `Continue`



Optional secondary actions:

\- `Review Objectives`

\- `Open Timeline`

\- `Open Priority Actions`



\## Design Rule

One strong forward action is enough.

This is a threshold, not a menu buffet.



---



\# 5A.11 Behaviour Rules



The Phase Transition Screen should appear:



\- at major phase boundaries,

\- after required gates are met,

\- before the player re-enters the main workflow.



It should:

\- feel rare enough to matter,

\- not overstay its welcome,

\- and always communicate something operationally useful.



---



\# 5A.12 Example Phase Transition Scenarios



\## Example 1. Entering Buyer Outreach

Message focus:

\- market-facing exposure begins

\- first impressions matter

\- materials readiness is now decisive



\## Example 2. Entering DD Phase

Message focus:

\- process becomes more demanding

\- buyer quality differences become visible

\- execution strain and issue risk rise sharply



\## Example 3. Entering Negotiation / Exclusivity

Message focus:

\- fewer moving parts, higher consequence

\- legal and timing risk dominate

\- seller fatigue and re-trade risk increase

\# 6. Screen Type B. Milestone Outcome Screen



---



\# 6B.1 Purpose



The Milestone Outcome Screen presents the result of a meaningful event, gate or process moment.



It should communicate:



\- what happened,

\- whether the outcome was positive, negative or mixed,

\- what changed because of it,

\- and what the player must now deal with.



This is where the system says:

the process reacted.



---



\# 6B.2 Primary Player Questions



The Milestone Outcome Screen must answer these questions immediately:



\- What just happened?

\- Was it good, bad or mixed?

\- What changed in practical terms?

\- Which metrics or systems moved?

\- What are the immediate implications?

\- What should I pay attention to next?



---



\# 6B.3 Functional Objectives



The Milestone Outcome Screen must support all of the following:



\- state the milestone result clearly,

\- summarise the outcome in plain terms,

\- show key consequences,

\- show metric and system changes,

\- link to follow-up actions or related screens,

\- and make result feedback satisfying and intelligible.



---



\# 6B.4 Screen Architecture



The Milestone Outcome Screen should behave as a \*\*full-screen overlay\*\*, \*\*modal event panel\*\*, or \*\*full-workspace event state\*\*, depending on severity and importance.



Recommended structure:



1\. Milestone Title Area

2\. Outcome Summary Block

3\. Consequence Breakdown

4\. Metric Delta Block

5\. New Priorities / Follow-Up Block

6\. Continue Action Area



Optional:

7\. Supporting visual state layer

8\. Linked entities or recommendations block



---



\# 6B.5 Milestone Title Area



\## Content

\- milestone name

\- result state

\- optional subtitle



Examples:

\- `Initial Buyer Feedback Received`

\- `Outcome: Mixed`

\- `Interest is present, but conviction is uneven.`



Or:

\- `Shortlist Confirmed`

\- `Outcome: Positive`

\- `Competitive tension preserved.`



\## Design Rule

This title must frame the emotional tone quickly and clearly.



---



\# 6B.6 Outcome Summary Block



\## Purpose

Provide a concise narrative explanation of the result.



\## Content

One or two short paragraphs describing:

\- what occurred,

\- what the result means,

\- and what tone the process now has.



Example:

\- `Buyer response has confirmed live interest, but not all early enthusiasm has translated into credible momentum. Two buyers appear serious, one is drifting and one has become more price-sensitive than expected.`



\## Design Rule

This summary should be interpretive, not generic.



---



\# 6B.7 Consequence Breakdown



\## Purpose

Explain the operational consequences of the result.



\## Content

Examples:

\- buyer heat increased

\- one bidder dropped

\- client confidence weakened slightly

\- shortlist pressure increased

\- DD readiness now urgent

\- timeline confidence reduced



This can be shown as:

\- concise bullet-style blocks

\- structured cards

\- or labelled rows



\## Design Rule

The player should not have to guess what changed.



---



\# 6B.8 Metric Delta Block



\## Purpose

Show which core systems moved.



\## Recommended Delta Types

Examples:

\- Deal Health

\- Client Confidence

\- Buyer Heat

\- Timeline Pressure

\- Risk Load

\- Team Capacity Stress

\- Deliverable Pressure



\## Presentation

Show:

\- metric name

\- direction

\- magnitude

\- optional short reason



Example:

\- `Client Confidence ↓ Moderate`

\- `Buyer Heat ↑ Strong`

\- `Timeline Pressure ↑ Slight`



\## Design Rule

This block should use crisp, compact visual logic.

This is where Terminal Prestige earns its keep.



---



\# 6B.9 New Priorities / Follow-Up Block



\## Purpose

Orient the player toward what matters now.



\## Content

Show 2 to 5 new priorities created by the outcome.



Examples:

\- prepare shortlist recommendation

\- address weak buyer signalling

\- reassure client on mixed external tone

\- improve DD readiness before next gate

\- investigate new legal concern



\## Behaviour

Each item may be clickable and link to the relevant screen.



---



\# 6B.10 Continue Action Area



\## Purpose

Move the player back into the simulation.



\## Content

Primary action:

\- `Continue`

or

\- `Return to Dashboard`



Optional secondary actions:

\- `Open Related Screen`

\- `Review Priorities`

\- `Open Timeline`



\## Design Rule

The screen should conclude cleanly.

No excessive branching here.



---



\# 6B.11 Result States



Milestone outcomes should support distinct tonal states:



\- Positive

\- Mixed Positive

\- Mixed

\- Mixed Negative

\- Negative

\- Critical



These should influence:

\- colour accents

\- phrasing

\- metric emphasis

\- and ambient tone



But always with restraint.



No fireworks for minor wins.

No opera for moderate pain.



---



\# 6B.12 Example Milestone Outcome Scenarios



\## Example 1. Teaser Launch Completed

Outcome:

\- positive if timely and high-quality

\- mixed if launched but with rushed materials



\## Example 2. Initial Buyer Feedback

Outcome:

\- mixed with uneven seriousness

\- positive if competitive tension is strong

\- negative if enthusiasm collapses early



\## Example 3. DD Red Flag Emerges

Outcome:

\- mixed negative or negative

\- risk load rises

\- buyer confidence and timeline confidence may fall



\## Example 4. Exclusivity Granted

Outcome:

\- positive if buyer credible and process controlled

\- mixed if certainty rises but leverage collapses

\- negative if granted under weakness



---



\# 7. Shared Progression Event Components



Both Phase Transition and Milestone Outcome screens should share reusable components.



\## Shared Components

\- Event Header

\- Summary Narrative Block

\- Impact / Consequence Cards

\- Metric Delta Row

\- Priority / Next Steps Block

\- Continue Footer

\- Optional related-links block



This creates visual continuity across progression events.



---



\# 8. Trigger Logic



\# 8.1 Phase Transition Triggers

Typical triggers:

\- entry into new phase

\- gate requirements satisfied

\- progression threshold met



\# 8.2 Milestone Outcome Triggers

Typical triggers:

\- completion of key milestone

\- arrival of result state

\- resolution of event cluster

\- end-of-week outcome with major consequence



\# 8.3 Design Rule

Progression event screens should be reserved for moments that deserve formal acknowledgement.

Too many, and they become wallpaper in a nice suit.



---



\# 9. Consequence Communication Rules



These screens must communicate consequences with discipline.



\## Required Principles

\- be concrete

\- be concise

\- be interpretable

\- link consequence to process state

\- orient the player toward next action



Avoid:

\- vague drama

\- abstract praise

\- generic failure language

\- empty “important things happened” fog



---



\# 10. Visual Hierarchy Rules



Both screen types should follow this hierarchy:



1\. Event identity, what this is

2\. Interpretation, what it means

3\. Consequences, what changed

4\. Forward orientation, what matters next

5\. Exit / continue action



That order matters.

Otherwise the player receives style before meaning, and style is a poor substitute for information no matter how shiny the gradients are.



---



\# 11. Motion and Presentation Rules



\# 11.1 Motion Principles

Motion should be:

\- restrained

\- elegant

\- slightly cinematic

\- brief



\# 11.2 Recommended Motion Types

\- soft fade-in

\- subtle panel reveal

\- controlled metric delta appearance

\- faint background atmosphere shift



\# 11.3 Avoid

\- explosive transitions

\- bouncy motion

\- overly dramatic flashes

\- arcade victory / failure energy



These are high-stakes business events, not pinball jackpots.



---



\# 12. Linked Systems Integration



These progression screens must connect directly to:



\- Dashboard

\- Timeline / Phase Map

\- Buyers

\- Client

\- Tasks \& Workstreams

\- Deliverables

\- Risks \& Issues

\- Inbox



Typical follow-up flow:

\- event occurs

\- progression screen summarises consequence

\- player continues into Dashboard or relevant system already changed by outcome



This is crucial.

The screen must not be a detached narrative postcard.



---



\# 13. Dynamic Behaviour



These screens should vary their content and emphasis based on:



\- current phase

\- severity of outcome

\- player performance

\- buyer state

\- client state

\- timing health

\- risk load

\- whether result is stabilising or destabilising the process



That allows the same milestone type to feel different across different runs.

As it should.



---



\# 14. UX States



These screens must support:



\- positive state

\- mixed state

\- negative state

\- critical state

\- light informational transition state

\- high-consequence event state



The degree of visual intensity should vary, but always remain disciplined.



---



\# 15. Accessibility and Readability



\# 15.1 Contrast

All result and phase states must remain readable in dark UI.



\# 15.2 Colour Usage

Do not rely only on colour to indicate outcome quality.

Use labels, wording and icons where appropriate.



\# 15.3 Scanability

The player should be able to extract:

\- what happened

\- what changed

\- what next

within seconds.



---



\# 16. Visual Style Guidance



\# 16.1 Dominant Variant

These screens should primarily use \*\*Cinematic Dealroom\*\*.



\# 16.2 Supporting Variant

Use \*\*Outlook Noir\*\* for:

\- structural clarity

\- text layout

\- action placement



Use \*\*Terminal Prestige\*\* for:

\- deltas

\- indicators

\- compact consequence logic



\# 16.3 Visual Rules

\- richer gradients than operational screens

\- more negative space

\- stronger event framing

\- restrained ambient glow

\- premium contrast

\- clear textual hierarchy

\- no theatrical clutter



These are the game’s formal chapter and consequence screens.

They should feel rare, elegant and precise.



---



\# 17. Example Combined Progression Flow



\## Example Flow

\- player clears final gate of Buyer Outreach phase

\- Phase Transition Screen appears:

&nbsp; - `Phase 3: Initial Feedback \& IOIs`

&nbsp; - explains that process now shifts from outbound momentum to market interpretation

&nbsp; - highlights shortlist logic and expectation management as key



Then, two weeks later:

\- Milestone Outcome Screen appears:

&nbsp; - `Initial Buyer Feedback Received`

&nbsp; - outcome mixed

&nbsp; - 2 buyers serious, 1 weak, 1 price-sensitive

&nbsp; - client confidence slightly down

&nbsp; - shortlist pressure up

&nbsp; - follow-up priorities created



This sequencing gives the game rhythm and memory.



---



\# 18. Initial Build Scope



For first playable implementation, include:



\- one shared event screen framework

\- Phase Transition mode

\- Milestone Outcome mode

\- title area

\- narrative summary block

\- consequence / impact block

\- metric delta row

\- priority / next steps block

\- primary continue action

\- optional related screen links



This is enough to make progression feel meaningfully staged.



---



\# 19. Future Enhancements



Possible later additions:

\- alternative outcome framing by client personality

\- richer event-specific visual motifs

\- replay log of major progression events

\- advisor commentary variations

\- event history archive

\- player performance comparison against planned process path

\- multiple tone variants depending on deal quality



Useful, yes.

Necessary now, no.



---



\# 20. Final Screen Statement



The Progression Events screens of \*\*The M\&A Rainmaker\*\* must function as a premium transition and consequence-feedback system, marking phase changes and milestone outcomes with clarity, structure and emotional restraint. They should give the player a strong sense that the deal is moving through meaningful chapters, and that each important result alters the shape, pressure and priorities of the process in a legible and memorable way.
