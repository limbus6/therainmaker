\# The M\&A Rainmaker — Buyer Generation Framework v1



\## 1. Purpose



This document defines the canonical buyer generation framework for \*\*The M\&A Rainmaker\*\*.



Its purpose is to ensure that buyers are not generic placeholders, but structured simulation actors generated with coherence across:



\* sector fit

\* process design fit

\* naming identity

\* bidder archetype

\* behaviour

\* pricing posture

\* diligence posture

\* certainty profile

\* message tone

\* late-stage outcomes



This framework should be read together with:



\* \*\*The\_MA\_Rainmaker\_Canonical\_Systems\_Spec\_v1.md\*\*

\* \*\*The\_MA\_Rainmaker\_Naming\_and\_Worldbuilding\_Framework.md\*\*

\* \*\*The\_MA\_Rainmaker\_Procedural\_Naming\_Library\_v1.md\*\*

\* \*\*The\_MA\_Rainmaker\_Recurring\_Institutions\_and\_Marquee\_Firms.md\*\*



The main design objective is simple. Buyers must feel like they were generated by a market logic, not by a random name generator suffering from caffeine instability.



---



\## 2. Design Principles



\## 2.1 Buyers are persistent actors



Every buyer should be a persistent simulation object with:



\* identity

\* motive

\* strengths

\* weaknesses

\* process preferences

\* behavioural patterns

\* memory of player interaction



Buyers should not merely appear to submit bids. They should evolve across the process.



\## 2.2 Buyers should be legible but not fully transparent



The player should be able to infer patterns from:



\* message tone

\* speed of engagement

\* quality of questions

\* diligence posture

\* bid structure

\* negotiation behaviour



The player should not see all underlying scores directly.



\## 2.3 Buyer generation must combine curated and procedural content



The framework should mix:



\* recurring marquee institutions

\* procedurally generated buyers

\* sector-specific strategic buyers

\* sponsor archetypes

\* occasional premium rivals



This provides both:



\* world continuity

&nbsp; n- replayability



\## 2.4 Buyers should support meaningful strategic trade-offs



The player should face real choices between buyers who differ on:



\* price

\* certainty

\* speed

\* strategic fit

\* diligence burden

\* legal aggression

\* process compatibility



The system must support situations where the highest apparent bid is not the best buyer.

\## 3. Buyer Taxonomy



\## 3.1 Canonical buyer classes



Use the following primary classes:



\* Strategic Buyer

\* Private Equity Buyer

\* Hybrid Buyer



\## 3.2 Strategic Buyer



A corporate acquirer motivated by:



\* synergies

\* strategic adjacency

\* market entry

\* capability acquisition

\* platform consolidation



Typical traits:



\* higher strategic narrative sensitivity

\* may pay up if fit is strong

\* integration logic matters

\* can be slower in governance-heavy organisations



\## 3.3 Private Equity Buyer



A financial sponsor motivated by:



\* value creation

\* platform logic

\* leverage and returns

\* buy-and-build potential

\* margin improvement or scale thesis



Typical traits:



\* more analytical scrutiny

\* more structured view on downside

\* stronger certainty differentiation across firms

\* often more sensitive to analytical weakness



\## 3.4 Hybrid Buyer



A buyer with mixed characteristics, such as:



\* family office with operating platform logic

\* sponsor-backed strategic vehicle

\* corporate investor with financial discipline

\* operating holding company



Typical traits:



\* can be less predictable

\* may have unusual strengths in certain sectors

\* useful for replayability and edge-case processes



---



\## 4. Buyer Generation Pipeline



\## 4.1 Canonical pipeline overview



Buyer generation should follow this order:



1\. Define deal context

2\. Define market envelope

3\. Determine buyer mix

4\. Pull recurring institution candidates

5\. Generate procedural candidates

6\. Assign archetypes

7\. Assign behavioural stats

8\. Assign naming and world identity

9\. Score process fit

10\. Select long list

11\. Seed hidden variation and edge cases



\## 4.2 Deal context inputs



Inputs should include:



\* sector

\* geography

\* size band

\* asset quality

\* growth profile

\* carve-out or standalone nature

\* complexity level

\* founder intensity

\* preparation quality

\* process design once chosen



\## 4.3 Market envelope



Before generating buyers, the engine should estimate:



\* how many strategic buyers plausibly exist

\* how many PE buyers plausibly exist

\* how globally attractive the asset is

\* whether the deal is broadable or niche

\* whether confidentiality risk is material

\* whether the asset is likely to draw premium curiosity or just practical bidders



This creates the universe from which actual buyers are selected.

\## 5. Buyer Archetype System



\## 5.1 Why archetypes matter



Archetypes provide behavioural identity beyond buyer class.



Two PE funds should not necessarily behave alike. Two strategic buyers should not necessarily negotiate alike.



\## 5.2 Canonical sponsor archetypes



\### 5.2.1 Premium Certainty Bidder



Typical profile:



\* disciplined

\* clean financing

\* strong governance

\* lower appetite for sloppy process execution



Usually:



\* strong certainty

\* moderate to strong price

\* low chaos



Examples:



\* Mithril Capital

\* selected premium procedural sponsors



\### 5.2.2 Dangerous Final-Round Bidder



Typical profile:



\* builds conviction well

\* sharp competitive instincts

\* capable of paying aggressively late

\* can also turn demanding once inside



Usually:



\* strong price upside

\* medium certainty

\* rising diligence intensity



Examples:



\* Ravenhold Capital



\### 5.2.3 High-Pressure Execution Bidder



Typical profile:



\* commercial

\* tough

\* fast when aligned

\* aggressive on downside protection



Usually:



\* solid pricing

\* medium certainty

\* strong legal pressure



Examples:



\* Crown Bastion Partners



\### 5.2.4 Quiet Reliable Bidder



Typical profile:



\* slower build

\* strong internal discipline

\* less flashy in process

\* often underrated by players chasing drama



Usually:



\* medium to strong price

\* high certainty

\* low chaos



Examples:



\* Greyhaven Equity



\### 5.2.5 Complexity-Loving Sponsor



Typical profile:



\* comfortable with carve-outs

\* analytical depth

\* willing to engage in messy situations

\* not for the faint of paperwork



Usually:



\* variable price

\* medium certainty

\* high diligence burden



Examples:



\* Obsidian Vale Partners



\## 5.3 Canonical strategic archetypes



\### 5.3.1 Synergy Maximalist



Typical profile:



\* pays up when fit is obvious

\* more sensitive to strategic story quality

\* often less tolerant of narrative inconsistency once exposed



\### 5.3.2 Governance-Heavy Corporate



Typical profile:



\* credible and serious

\* slower process

\* more approvals

\* strong certainty if it gets through gates



\### 5.3.3 Fast-Moving Challenger



Typical profile:



\* energetic

\* opportunistic

\* can move quickly

\* not always institutionally clean at the end



\### 5.3.4 Defensive Consolidator



Typical profile:



\* disciplined strategic rationale

\* modest excitement

\* good fit for scale stories

\* strong pricing discipline



\### 5.3.5 Capability Hunter



Typical profile:



\* interested in talent, IP or specific capability

\* may care less about broad platform story

\* can be strong in niche sectors



\## 5.4 Hybrid archetypes



\### 5.4.1 Sponsor-Backed Strategic



\### 5.4.2 Industrial Holding Platform



\### 5.4.3 Family Office with Operator DNA



\### 5.4.4 Mission-Led Strategic Investor



These should be less common and used for texture or advanced scenarios.

\## 7. Behavioural Attribute Definitions



\## 7.1 Interest



Initial attraction to the asset.



\## 7.2 Conviction



Depth of belief formed through process exposure.



\## 7.3 Process Fit



How well the buyer suits the chosen process design and auction structure.



\## 7.4 Pricing Bias



Structural tendency to pay strongly if engaged.



\## 7.5 Speed



How fast the buyer progresses through stages when sufficiently interested.



\## 7.6 Certainty



Likelihood of moving from serious interest to signable outcome cleanly.



\## 7.7 Diligence Aggressiveness



Intensity of questions, DD scope and issue extraction behaviour.



\## 7.8 Legal Aggressiveness



How hard the buyer pushes in SPA and risk allocation.



\## 7.9 Confidentiality Sensitivity



How much leak risk and process visibility affect willingness to engage.



\## 7.10 Narrative Sensitivity



How strongly the buyer reacts to equity story quality and strategic positioning.



\## 7.11 Analytical Sensitivity



How strongly the buyer reacts to weaknesses in numbers, model or data room.



\## 7.12 Governance Friction



How much internal approval structure slows or complicates process progression.

\## 9. Process Design Interaction Rules



\## 9.1 Broad Auction



Effects on buyer generation:



\* larger long list

\* wider quality spread

\* more opportunistic buyers included

\* stronger role for speed and teaser response

\* higher leak exposure for confidentiality-sensitive buyers



Favours:



\* fast-moving challengers

\* dangerous final-round bidders

\* synergy maximalists if strategic narrative is strong



Penalises:



\* highly confidentiality-sensitive corporates

\* governance-heavy buyers with slow internal processes



\## 9.2 Targeted Auction



Effects:



\* smaller, better-fitted buyer pool

\* stronger average fit

\* lower noise

\* better relationship quality potential



Favours:



\* premium certainty bidders

\* governance-heavy corporates

\* capability hunters

\* quiet reliable bidders



\## 9.3 Pre-emptive Strategy



Effects:



\* very small buyer pool

\* stronger weighting to certainty and chemistry

\* pricing ceiling lower unless one buyer fit is exceptional



Favours:



\* high-certainty sponsors

\* strong strategic matches

\* fast-moving corporates with clear rationale



Penalises:



\* buyers that rely on competitive tension to stretch



---



\## 10. Preparation Quality Interaction Rules



Buyers should be affected by Phase 1 preparation quality.



\## 10.1 Strong teaser



Increases:



\* initial interest

\* NDA conversion

\* early narrative trust



\## 10.2 Strong IM



Increases:



\* conviction build rate

\* strategic fit clarity

\* buyer retention in mid-process



\## 10.3 Strong financial model



Increases:



\* sponsor confidence

\* analytical resilience

\* final round trust

\* lower retrade risk



\## 10.4 Strong data room



Increases:



\* DD efficiency

\* certainty

\* lower withdrawal risk

\* lower fatigue accumulation



\## 10.5 Weak preparation



Creates:



\* lower conversion

\* reduced conviction

\* more noise buyers and curiosity-only interest

\* higher diligence aggression later

\* higher retrade and dropout risk

\## 11.2 Stage progression principle



Progression should depend on:



\* stage eligibility

\* generated buyer profile

\* current relationship state

\* quality of materials

\* player responsiveness

\* broader process pressure

\* event outcomes



\## 11.3 Buyer fatigue



A buyer should accumulate fatigue when:



\* Q\&A is slow

\* materials are weak

\* process delays drag on

\* deadlines move too often

\* seller communication becomes inconsistent



High fatigue should increase:



\* withdrawal risk

\* pricing discipline

\* legal protectiveness

\* reduced excitement

\## 12. Buyer Memory System



\## 12.1 Purpose



Buyers need memory so that process interactions matter.



\## 12.2 Canonical memory inputs



Track counters such as:



\* deadlines missed

\* quick responses received

\* management impressed

\* diligence friction

\* quality of materials

\* confidentiality concerns

\* legal friction

\* transparency quality



\## 12.3 Effects of memory



Memory should modify:



\* conviction trajectory

\* warmth

\* certainty

\* bid competitiveness

\* retrade risk

\* withdrawal likelihood



A buyer that repeatedly experienced sloppy execution should not behave as though nothing happened. That would be simulation amnesia, and that disease spreads fast.

\## 13. Bid Logic Framework



\## 13.1 First-round logic



NBO likelihood and NBO quality should depend on:



\* interest

\* conviction

\* strategic or financial fit

\* quality of preparation

\* process design

\* management chemistry where relevant



\## 13.2 Final offer logic



Final offers should depend on:



\* surviving conviction

\* diligence outcomes

\* price ceiling from archetype

\* competitive tension

\* certainty penalty or premium

\* fatigue

\* trust



\## 13.3 Bid components



A bid should not be only a number. It may include:



\* headline price level

\* structure quality

\* certainty level

\* diligence conditions

\* financing robustness

\* timing credibility

\* legal aggressiveness expectation

\## 16. Hidden Edge Cases and Texture Rules



\## 16.1 Curiosity-only buyers



Some buyers may engage early but never become serious. Useful for realism.



\## 16.2 Prestige-only buyers



Some buyers enter because the asset is fashionable, not because they truly fit.



\## 16.3 Internal-politics buyers



Some strategic buyers may look strong but suffer high governance friction.



\## 16.4 Relationship-driven buyers



Some buyers overperform if management chemistry is strong.



\## 16.5 Fragile conviction buyers



Some bidders look strong until one DD issue collapses confidence.



These edge cases should be used sparingly to maintain texture.

\## 17. Vanilla JavaScript Generation Outline



```js

function generateBuyerPool(dealContext, worldState) {

&nbsp; const marketEnvelope = buildMarketEnvelope(dealContext);

&nbsp; const candidateInstitutions = pullRecurringInstitutionCandidates(dealContext, worldState);

&nbsp; const proceduralBuyers = generateProceduralCandidates(dealContext, marketEnvelope, worldState);

&nbsp; const allCandidates = \[...candidateInstitutions, ...proceduralBuyers];



&nbsp; const enriched = allCandidates.map(candidate => {

&nbsp;   const archetype = assignBuyerArchetype(candidate, dealContext);

&nbsp;   const stats = assignBuyerStats(candidate, archetype, dealContext);

&nbsp;   const name = candidate.name || generateBuyerName(candidate, dealContext, worldState);

&nbsp;   return {

&nbsp;     ...candidate,

&nbsp;     ...stats,

&nbsp;     archetype,

&nbsp;     name

&nbsp;   };

&nbsp; });



&nbsp; return selectLongList(enriched, dealContext, marketEnvelope);

}

```

\## 19. Content Design Rules Summary



1\. Buyers are simulation actors, not decorative names.

2\. Use sector fit first, then naming style, then behavioural variance.

3\. Combine recurring institutions with procedural entrants.

4\. Buyer archetypes must produce distinct trade-offs.

5\. Process design must change which buyers thrive.

6\. Preparation quality must materially affect buyer behaviour.

7\. Buyer memory must make interactions consequential.

8\. Final bids must reflect price, certainty and structure, not price alone.

9\. Message tone should reinforce buyer identity.

10\. Hidden edge cases should add texture, not chaos.



---



\## 20. Final Note



A strong buyer generation system is one of the central engines of replayability in \*\*The M\&A Rainmaker\*\*.



If buyers feel generic, every auction becomes the same auction wearing a different tie.



If buyers feel distinct, remembered and causally responsive, the player starts reading the market as a living field of personalities, motives and risk.



That is where the game stops being a sequence of screens and starts becoming a professional world.
